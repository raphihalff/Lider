For most of the ten days that I was in Paris it was an exercise in not disturbing the volcano.  My father would cry from morning to night of how he had been betrayed.   And yet it seemed obvious that he was the one doing the betraying.  More and more my father seemed less than a man and more like a child.  We would be out in public and my father would not stop sniping at his ex-wife.   He had to yell at someone and she was the easiest target.  Why she stood for it I don’t know.  I think she realizes that he is sick.  I did get a chance to speak to my ex-Chinese step-mother.  We had lunch together while my father and his wife went to the bank.  I had become disappointed in her because she had gone to Bosnia and had been baptized as a Catholic.  She says that being a Chinese Catholic will help her in her business.  But I was simply appalled.  She used to care so much for my father’s Judaism and her own Buddhism.  But at this lunch she said some things that really made sense.   I had brought up the subject of the homeless people I work with.  How they don’t get along socially and wind up homeless and how my father resembles this scenario.  And she said that my father could never get along with anyone in France whether they were teachers or potential employers.  (He spent 17 years getting degrees from the Sorbonne and never apparently had an income.)  In China and Mongolia, however, my father was accepted.  Accepted as being a foreigner that has mastered their language.  Somehow my father is able to get along in Asia in a way he was not able to in the West.  I suppose this is something to be admired in my father.  Instead of being marginalized in one society he found a way of existing in another. Of course others have paved his way to do so, though he would never admit to this.  And the Holocaust is further away in the East, though the Nazi episode took place in a Mongolian hospital.

 It was a good piece of luck that the Chinese wife had her student along for the time that I was there.  The student is an archeologist whose specialty is restoration of artifacts.  I had an opportunity to climb the oldest tower in Paris which has been closed to the public for four years for restoration.  We took a tour of her office and saw Roman artifacts in the process of being restored.  One night the student wanted to treat us to a concert in a church and dinner.  Before the concert we went to the Luxembourg Gardens and to the Museum Carnavalet which is a museum dedicated to the history of Paris.  We walked a good deal that night.  My father could not take some time and appreciate any of these activities.  All he could think of was his own concerns.  One morning I could not take any more of his crying over his residency card (which he got without a problem) and I said “Genghis Khan did not worry over residency cards”.  I got a big laugh from the three women in the apartment. 

The student also drove us to my father’s country house about an hour west from Paris. This was yet another abode his ex-wife had to maintain financially in my father’s name. I believe he will sign the house over to his daughter.  When we got to the house tired from a day of errands, my father insisted that we clear out the basement of boxes and trunks and put them in the garage.  In the basement were about 10,000 books mostly in Chinese and French.  The books were in beautifully made glass enclosed bookcases made by my brother’s brother-in-law before the fiasco.  On the basement floor were metal trunks and boxes filled with every scrap of paper ever collected by my father’s family.  They (or rather my father) kept everything.  I was reminded of my father’s sisters who also were “packrats”.  The Chinese wife called a couple of friends from a nearby church and paid them to move the stuff.

 We finished at 10:00 at night.   We then all went to the only restaurant in town, run by a family from Naples, Italy.  We each had our own sumptuous pizza.  At 11:30 we all drove to a nearby village where the Chinese wife holds workshops.  She is part of a weird Catholic group devoted to “children”.  We sat around the table and my father’s Mongolian wife was asked to sing to us some Mongolian “long songs”.  The Mongolian wife stood up and in a lovely voice began to sing, but to my horror my father sitting next to her, began to accompany her in the singing.  We all wanted to hear her singing but my father in his typically rude fashion would not or could not see this.  When we returned to the country house, I went to sleep in my sister’s bed which has remained untouched for many years.

 Two of the days that I was in Paris I was forced to go to the National Library of France.  My father was obsessed with this library.  He had a card and his wife had a card (obtained on a previous visit to Paris) and his task was to get me a research card.  You have to be interviewed in order to obtain a card.  I wanted to say that my research subject was yizkor books (the library had 40 of them), but my father insisted on listing me as his Mongolian studies research assistant. I had to wait an hour to be interviewed, and was embarrassed by the whole ordeal.   I do have to admit that the library building was amazing, maybe the largest library in the world.  My father ordered some books to be brought to him, but he did not do much work.   I think he just was so impressed with himself that he could be in such a great place.   While at the library my father’s wife used the internet to find organizations that would use her husband’s consulting services in Mongolia.  My father always has a way of getting others to work for him.

I don’t know if I will see my father again.  I did say that I would go to Mongolia in two years.  First I want to return to Israel and close that chapter.  Going to Mongolia would validate my father further.  Going over there I would feel that I was humoring an old man.  Drinking a Bloody Mary with my father one day, he began to sing a famous swing tune called “St. James Infirmary”.  I know this song and sang it with him.  He ran away from me and from America, but you can never hide completely.  One’s childhood seeps through.  After singing the song I think my father began to realize what he lost in me.   We could have been singing all of these years.  But it was on my next to last day in Paris that I myself felt my mission was accomplished.  I put on the stereo a recording of Bach violin music.  The violin playing was arresting.  I had never heard music like this before.  Perhaps I knew that something special was in store for me when I chose that compact disc to play.  I sat down next to my father and I remember saying to myself, “this is the sound of redemption”.   We talked about the portrait of his grandfather on the mantle in front of us.  Who was he?  How did he live?   And as the violin hit impossible combination of notes, I knew I was in the right place on the couch with my father.

Dear Khavar,

I might not see you again after I leave Paris.  Therefore I want to write you this letter so that I may tell you about my life and history as it relates to Mikhail.  You said to me that Mikhail has told you about his first wife-my mother.  This bothers me because Mikhail usually sees the past in a very different way from what others see this same past.  I should be the one to tell you about Mikhail's first marriage NOT Mikhail.  I hope you trust me with this.

I am surprised that Mikhail has told you about his life growing up in America.  Mikhail was never honest with Kar Fung and their two sons about his past.  This caused a lot of problems between us.  Mikhail never told his sons that he was from America.  He told them that he was some sort of Russian.  Mikhail refused to teach his sons English so that he could speak to KF in a language that his sons could not understand.  Mikhail also told Kar Fung many false things about his early life.  Mikhail told Kar Fung that his father died when he was a child.  This is untrue as Mikhail's father, Solomon Leash (Lishnofsky), died in 1968.  I am sure though that Mikhail did not have much of a reletionship with his father.

As I am on the subject of Mikhail's father I will have to address the issue of my heritage and ancestry.  While it is true that Mikhail's father was from South Russia (Crimea, Ukraine), I can assure you that he was a Jewish man from Russia, without a connection to Mongolia.  I respect Mikhail when he says that he feels Mongolian "in his blood".  What I will NOT accept is Mikhail changing MY family history for his own ends.  Perhaps centuries ago Mongols/Khazars converted to Judaism and my ancestry has Asian roots.  But as far as the last four or five generations, I can assure you that my ancestors were simple people, born in Russia as Jews.  This is nothing to be ashamed of although I feel that Mikhail wishes so much to prove that he is connected to the Mongolian people that he has invented his own ancestry.  Mikhail was born Myron Leash in Detroit in 1939.

My mother was born Arlene Swartzberg in 1930.  Arlene grew up on Long Island, New York.  My mother is Jewish but she is not religeous.  Arlene is a secular (non-religeous) Jew, as is Mikhail.  Arlene went to a dance studio in New York City to take dancing lessons from a famous dancer named Charles Weidman.  Mikhail worked with Weidman and this is where my parents met.  My parents were married in 1964.  I was born Steven Santaro in 1966 in New York City.

Mikhail worked as a translator and as a flight attendant for now defunct TWA (airlines).  It was my mother who supported the family financially as much of the time Mikhail was studying Chinese and DID NOT WORK.  My mother worked as an advertising writer.  My mother also worked hard typing for my father his translation work.  So you see, Mikhail has ALWAYS had women do his work!

As a small child I felt very close to my father.  Unfortunately my father was away for long periods of time causing me heartache.Through his job with the airline he was able to go to Hong Kong.  In 1970 Mikhail met Kar Fung in Hong Kong.  My mother decided to follow Mikhail to Hong Kong to try to keep the family together.  In Hong Kong Mikhail took my mother to meet some British Jewish family so that they might hep my mother and I. This Jewish family recommended that my mother and I emmigrate Israel.  Israel is a Zionist State.  This means that all Jews can move to Israel and become citizens.  My mother and I moved to Israel in 1971.  Mikhail ASSURED my mother that he would follow us and live in Israel as a Jew and open up an acupuncture clinic.  I do not know whether my father actually believed that he would do this or that it was a lie from the beginning. 

As a side note I would like to say something about the photographs you saw at Saacy showing Mikhail and Kar Fung in Israel in 1971. I also saw these photographs in 1996 (or 1993).  I discussed the pictures with my mother and she said that Mikhail did visit us in Israel in 1971 but did not tell my mother that he had brought Kar Fung with him on the trip.  This was a rotten thing for Mikhail to do. 

Anyway Bianling was born in 1972.  Mikhail did not seek a divorce until 1975. 

Let me tell you something about my childhood in Israel as most of my trauma occurred there.  I was an American going to school with Israeli children.  I was a little smarter than most children and so I was bullied a lot. Since you told me of your own childhood traumas being a Mongolian minority in a Chinese school, you should understand how it was. My mother never learned Hebrew-the language of Israel.  My mother always worked, mostly as an English language secretary.  I do not know the number of times Mikhail came to Israel.  I believe it was 2 or 3 times.  In 1974 I lived with Mikhail and Kar Fung in Brittanny, France for 6 months.  In 1974 Mikhail came to Israel for a divorce.  In reality my parents had been separated for years.  My father took me for a walk to tell me that now the separation was final.  I remember becoming hysterical (very upset). 

MIKHAIL NEVER PAID MY MOTHER ANY CHILD SUPPORT MONEY EVER. 

I have been seeing a psychologist for four years now.  I have two talk therapy sessions a week.  I will tell you now of two events I discuss often with my therapist which occurred in Israel.  During one of Mikhail's visits to Israel we took a trip to Jerusalem.  I was 7 or 8 years old.  My father and I were to spend a couple of days in Jerusalem.  However on the day of our arrival, something inexplicable (to a child) happened.  Mikhail made other plans and sent me back to my mother.  I had to take a train back to the city of Haifa by myself for two hours.  When I arrived I had to locate my mother at her work which I could not find for hours.  Basically Mikhail abandoned me once more, perhaps because he met a woman, I don't know.

The second event I speak often about with my therapist is when Mikhail bought some shoelaces in Israel and placed me on a street corner in Israel to sell these shoelaces.  For what reason?  To teach me a lesson?  A few days ago you told me a story Mikhail told you about being 9 years old and shining shoes for money.  I really do not believe this story but I will have to ask his brothers.  Anyway even if this did happen to Mikhail it was wrong to humiliate his son in this way.

Khavar, you already know that a son needs a father.  You know what has happened to Tuo Ling.  Bian Ling too has suffered for not having a capable father.  Perhaps you are enough of a caretaker for my sister that she has not suffered as bad as her brothers have.

A few more facts.  In 1982 Mikhail visited my mother and me in Chicago. Mikhail had been invited to talk at an acupuncture school in Chicago. I had not seen him in years.  I was 16 years old and deperately needed a connection with my father.  But all Mikhail did was study his Mongolian dictionary.  Mikhail would not speak to me.  In 1985 I visited the Santaro family in Ivry.  I was unable to communicate with my brothers because Mikhail had not taught them English.  In 1993 I tried again to establish something with Mikhail.  However I found myself in the middle of a fight between Mikhail and Tuo Ling, so I left soon after arriving.

I have suffered from clinical depression all of my life.  It is only in the last couple of years, with the help of talk therapy and medicine, that I have been able to lead a more normal life without depression crippling me.

You may wonder about my romantic life.  I have never had a girlfiend.  I am very afraid of rejection.  This stems from my feeling rejected as a child.  I do not blame Mikhail entirely, but having a father may have helped me deal better with women.

I have told you that all 5 of Mikhail's sisters suffer from some sort of mental psychosis.  You can verify this easily.  Unfortunately Tuo Ling seems to have inherited this family disease.  I leave it up to you to determine if Mikhail suffers similarly. 

I must go on knowing that Mikhail will never be the person I would like him to be. 

It is up to you if you want to share this letter with my sister.

Sincerely,
